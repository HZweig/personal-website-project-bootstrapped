Tu B'Shvat, the holiday known as the New Year for Trees. If you are looking for multiple ways to indulge in Tu B'Shvat we have you covered. Learn about the seven traditional fruits that are eaten on Tu B'Shvat. Tu B'Shvat provides a great opportunity to connect with your local Jewish community through tree plantings and other holiday festivities.

Tu B'Shvat is not just for kids, and it's not eating fruit and nuts, and it's not just or even, or only, or necessarily "the four worlds", which is a Kabbalistic frame that may be useful to you, or it may not. Make your own little Tu B'Shvat seder. Have some special food. Ask some questions. We have a bunch of resources on our website and you should feel free to read stuff, download stuff, adapt things. But most of all: really celebrate Tu B'Shvat. It's the full moon of shvat. It's winter. We hope that spring will come. We hope the sun will shine; we hope the earth will be bountiful; we hope and pray and intend that our own behaviors will help leave this place in better shape for those who come after us.

I leave you with a poem that I haven't read in aeons, but randomly felt like googling to close this email with. It has become, I think, quite well-known. I commend it to you for your Tu B'Shvat seder this year. This is Mary Oliver:

You do not have to be good.
You do not have to walk on your knees
for a hundred miles through the desert repenting.
You only have to let the soft animal of your body
love what it loves.
Tell me about despair, yours, and I will tell you mine.
Meanwhile the world goes on.
Meanwhile the sun and the clear pebbles of the rain
are moving across the landscapes,
over the prairies and the deep trees,
the mountains and the rivers.
Meanwhile the wild geese, high in the clean blue air,
are heading home again.
Whoever you are, no matter how lonely,
the world offers itself to your imagination,
calls to you like the wild geese, harsh and exciting -
over and over announcing your place
in the family of things.
